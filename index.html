Exploring Named Entity Recognition on Repertorium Germanicum I

How do NER models with different linguistic and historical training backgrounds process highly abbreviated Latin regesta?

Yiyang Huang ·

View repository

Abstract

This project explores the use of named entity recognition (NER) on the Latin regesta of Repertorium Germanicum I. These texts differ substantially from the modern, standardized language on which many NER systems are trained: they are highly compressed, contain frequent abbreviations, and include inflected or Latinized forms of personal and geographical names.

This project applies three pretrained NER models to text segments extracted from the XML and compares their outputs. Each model represents a different training context: general Latin, medieval documentary Latin, and modern multilingual NER. The aim is not to identify a definitively “best” model, but to investigate how different NER systems respond to the linguistic and editorial challenges of historical regesta.

The Corpus

Repertorium Germanicum I

The Repertorium Germanicum, produced by the German Historical Institute in Rome, records people, churches, and places connected with the German-speaking territories of the Holy Roman Empire as they appear in Vatican registers. This project uses its first volume,RG I, available through Repertorium Germanicum Online.

RG contains regesta rather than complete transcriptions. These concise scholarly summaries preserve essential information about people, places, institutions, dates, and legal actions without reproducing the complete wording of the original documents.

A Textual Example

“Conrado dicto Schilher ecclesiam par. in Tronstat Herbip. dioc.”

This short extract contains a personal name, a place name, and several abbreviations. It demonstrates the compressed and entity-dense character of the regesta. Such features are common across the corpus: abbreviations occur in 87.9% of all extracted segments. Historical and Latinized names, abbreviated contexts, and compressed syntax all present potential challenges for NER.

From XML to Workable Data

The XML was parsed into 9,434 individual text segments: 3,845 heads and 5,589 sublemmata belonging to 3,845 lemmas. XML markup for abbreviations, dates, and source references was extracted into separate tables.

For the NER analysis, structured source references were removed, while original spelling, punctuation, dates, and abbreviations were preserved. Each head and sublemma was then stored as an individual row, producing a consistent tabular dataset that could be processed by all three models.
